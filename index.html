Why

Story Mapping?

Because flat backlogs are terrible at communicating the big pictures or systems.

Flat backlogs are great at communicating a detailed view of what will be completed at the end of the sprint. But it doesn't help us communicate or visualize how that sprint fits or adds to the over all systems or software we are building.

It's a big pile of context free stuff the SOMEBODY is making us build. With flat backlogs we can see how a user gets from point A to point B but it is impossible to see they get from point A to point Z.

It's a better way to build backlogs.

Creating feature after feature and adding it to a backlog is a great way to become a feature factory. Mindlessly adding complexity to a system without any real understand of why and how it beneifits the end user.

It help us see how to gradulally build up the systems and only focus on the current and next MVP while at the same time seeing the big picture.

Also because written requirements alone are a terrible way to build ANYTHING!

How does a story map help?

Shows the whole system from the users perspective

Created from a broad to detail view

Keeps the focus on end user needs

Organize roadmaps, releases, &
minimal viable products

Discover holes in the assumed workflows

Discover feature waste

Better way to discover business value

Gets the team & shareholders talking & collaborating

Who should be involved in making the story map?

Everybody of course

Well kind of...

The Product Owner Team should start the story map

Bring in technical, testing, & UI/UX resources to help create release groups

Invite Sales & Marketing to do a walk-through

Anybody that can Fire you, do the walk-through

Anybody that you think can Fire you but are not sure about, do the walk-through

How do you create a story map?

Use Fast, Cheap, & Disposable methods of communication
(i.e. Cards, Markers, Stickys, Sketches, Pictures)

Photo of markers and cards

Create user Personas

Identify the user Goals

Determine the user Activities that can achieve those goals from left to right

Ascertain the user Tasks that will complete those activities ordered from top to bottom

Goals: Manage Emails, Manage Calendars, Manage Contacts Activities: Read emails, Send Email, Delete Email, Configure Accounts Tasks: List Emails, Add one Pop3 account, Add one IMap account, Manage Accounts, Add Multiple Pop3, Add Multiple IMap Accounts.

Aren't those just Epics, Stories, & Tasks?

NO

...but they are related

Split user tasks into smaller tasks

Look for missing & wasteful tasks

All of this should be created from lots & lots of Conversations

Separate tasks into Release groups

Walk people through the system.
QA, Developers, Sales, Marketing, the CEO, your Boss, that guy over there...

Now What?

Top slice should be a balance of thin but comprehensive

Thin enough to make the work simple

Comprehensive enough for DevOps to make a deployable product

Move the next slice into a backlog & start grooming!

Before that slice is complete refine the next slice

Repeat

What do you do with a Story Map when you are done?

Ahhhh.....

As long you are supporting, maintaining, or working on the project

Work only 3-4 release groups ahead

Put a done sticky on completed tasks

Date a slice when it is released

Feed it with new ideas & work

Change it when those ideas do or don't work

Talk about the new ideas & work

Make it the single most important information radiator next to the Teams Sprint Wall

User Story Mapping

The book!

User story mapping is a valuable tool for software development, once you understand why and how to use it. This insightful book examines how this often misunderstood technique can help your team stay focused on users and their needs without getting lost in the enthusiasm for individual product features.

Author Jeff Patton shows you how changeable story maps enable your team to hold better conversations about the project throughout the development process. Your team will learn to come away with a shared understanding of what you’re attempting to build and why.
